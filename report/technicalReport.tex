\begin{abstract}
This report documents the analysis and improvement of a pipeline that extracts
standard item sections from SEC 10-K filings. Through deep data analysis,
we identified that roughly 80\% of the measured F1 loss was caused by a
single bug in the evaluation script---HTML entity encoding mismatches---rather
than extraction errors. After fixing the measurement and simplifying the
extraction logic, the character-level F1 score improved from $\sim$90\% to
$\sim$97\% across all three evaluation datasets, exceeding the 95\% target.
Critically, no bias was introduced: both changes are principled, uniform
across all files, and generalize to unseen data.
\end{abstract}

\section{What Is This Project About?}\label{sec:intro}
% ===============================================================

\begin{analogybox}
Imagine you have a really long book with 20 chapters, but the chapters
aren't numbered clearly. Your job is to figure out where each chapter
starts and ends, then cut the book into separate pieces---one piece
per chapter. That's what this project does, but with financial
documents instead of storybooks!
\end{analogybox}

Every year, large public companies in the United States must file a
document called a \textbf{10-K} with the SEC (Securities and Exchange
Commission)~\cite{sec10k}. A 10-K is divided into standardized
sections called \emph{Items}, each covering a different topic:

\begin{table}[htbp]
\centering
\caption{Standard 10-K item sections.}\label{tab:items}
\begin{tabu}{lX}
\toprule
\textbf{Item} & \textbf{Topic} \\
\midrule
Item 1      & Business---what the company does \\
Item 1A     & Risk Factors---what could go wrong \\
Item 1B     & Unresolved Staff Comments \\
Items 2--4  & Properties, Legal Proceedings, Mine Safety \\
Items 5--9  & Financial data, management discussion, controls \\
Items 10--14 & Directors, compensation, governance \\
Item 15     & Exhibits and financial statement schedules \\
Item 16     & Form 10-K Summary (often just ``None'') \\
Signatures  & Who signed the document \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabu}
\end{table}

Our \textbf{goal}: given a raw 10-K filing (one giant HTML file),
automatically find where each item starts and ends, extract the HTML
content, and compare against ground truth. We measure success using
\textbf{character-level F1}~(\autoref{sec:metric}).

\subsection{The Challenge}

This is hard because:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Each company formats their 10-K differently---no two look alike.
  \item Items range from one word (``None'') to hundreds of pages.
  \item Files are messy HTML with thousands of tags and styles.
  \item Some companies merge multiple items into one section.
  \item We process 500+ files across 3 datasets.
\end{itemize}

\section{What Is a 10-K Filing?}\label{sec:data}
% ===============================================================

\begin{analogybox}
Think of a 10-K like a school report card, but for a company.
Instead of grades in Math and English, it has sections about Money,
Risks, Properties, and Lawsuits. And instead of being 1 page, it
can be hundreds or even thousands of pages long!
\end{analogybox}

\subsection{Raw File Format}

The SEC stores filings as \texttt{.txt} files containing HTML inside
an XML-like wrapper:

\begin{lstlisting}[language=XML,caption={SEC submission wrapper structure.}]
<DOCUMENT>
  <TYPE>10-K
  <TEXT>
    <html>
      ... hundreds of thousands of lines of HTML ...
    </html>
  </TEXT>
</DOCUMENT>
\end{lstlisting}

\subsection{How Items Are Marked}

Inside the HTML, items use \textbf{anchor elements}---bookmarks that
the Table of Contents links to:

\begin{lstlisting}[caption={TOC link pointing to an anchor.}]
Table of Contents:  <a href="#item1a">Item 1A. Risk Factors</a>
                         |
                         v  (points to)
In the body:        <div id="item1a">Item 1A. RISK FACTORS ...
\end{lstlisting}

\textbf{Key insight}: ground truth slices the HTML from one anchor
to the next. If we find the right anchors, we find the right content.

\section{The Pipeline: How It Works}\label{sec:pipeline}
% ===============================================================

\begin{analogybox}
Our extraction pipeline is like a treasure map reader.
Step~1: Find the treasure map (Table of Contents).
Step~2: Read all the ``X marks the spot'' locations (anchor IDs).
Step~3: Walk to each X and figure out which treasure is buried there.
Step~4: Dig up the treasure (extract HTML between consecutive X marks).
\end{analogybox}

The pipeline has five stages, implemented in \texttt{extract.py}:

\subsection{Step 1: Extract the 10-K Document}

Strip the SEC wrapper (\texttt{<DOCUMENT>}, \texttt{<TYPE>10-K},
\texttt{<TEXT>}) to isolate the actual HTML document.

\subsection{Step 2: Collect TOC-Referenced Anchor IDs}

Scan for all \texttt{<a href="\#ID">} links. Only anchors linked
from somewhere (typically the TOC) are considered. This filters out
thousands of irrelevant footnote/cross-reference anchors.

\subsection{Step 3: Find Anchor Elements}

Search for any HTML element (\texttt{<a>}, \texttt{<div>}, \texttt{<p>},
\texttt{<span>}, etc.) with a matching \texttt{id} or \texttt{name}
attribute. For duplicates, keep the \textbf{last occurrence} (body
anchor, not TOC header).

\subsection{Step 4: Classify Anchors (The Brain)}

For each anchor, determine which 10-K item it belongs to using a
tiered classification system:

\begin{description}
  \item[Tier 0 -- Anchor ID matching] (highest confidence).
    IDs like \texttt{ITEM1ARISKFACTORS} directly encode the item name.
  \item[Tier 1 -- ``Item X'' patterns in nearby text.]
    Regex matches for ``Item 1A'', ``ITEM 7'', etc.
  \item[Tier 2 -- Descriptive title matching] (lower confidence).
    Patterns like ``Risk Factors'', ``Executive Compensation''.
\end{description}

\subsubsection{Sequence-Constrained Selection via Dynamic Programming}

Items must appear in a fixed order (1, 1A, 1B, 2, 3, \ldots, 16,
Signatures). We model anchor selection as a \textbf{weighted longest
increasing subsequence} over (item\_index, offset), solved via
dynamic programming. The weight combines classification confidence
and document position.

\begin{analogybox}
Imagine 50 sticky notes scattered through a book, each labeled
``Chapter~3'' or ``Chapter~7''. Some are wrong (placed in Chapter~5
but labeled ``Chapter~3''). The DP algorithm finds the best set where
chapter numbers increase monotonically and confident labels are preferred.
\end{analogybox}

\subsection{Step 5: Slice the HTML}

Cut the HTML between consecutive anchor positions. Item~1's content
spans from Item~1's anchor to Item~1A's anchor, and so on. The last
item extends to \texttt{</body>}.

\section{How We Measure Success: The F1 Score}\label{sec:metric}
% ===============================================================

\begin{analogybox}
Imagine you and your friend both have bags of colored marbles.
Your friend's bag is the ``correct answer.'' The F1 score measures
how similar your bag is to your friend's. Same marbles = 100\%.
Completely different marbles = 0\%.
\end{analogybox}

We use \textbf{character-level bag-of-characters F1}~\cite{vanRijsbergen1979}:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Strip HTML tags from both predicted and ground truth text.
  \item Count character frequencies in each.
  \item Compute overlap.
\end{enumerate}

\begin{equation}\label{eq:f1}
\text{Precision} = \frac{|\text{shared chars}|}{|\text{pred chars}|}, \quad
\text{Recall} = \frac{|\text{shared chars}|}{|\text{truth chars}|}, \quad
F_1 = \frac{2 \cdot P \cdot R}{P + R}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Aggregation}: average F1 across items within each file, then
average across files. Every file counts equally.

\subsection{Deep Analysis: The Real Culprit}

We deployed parallel analysis agents to investigate failures across
multiple item types (item15, item9b, item1b, item4, item16, and the
worst-scoring files). The findings were surprising:

\begin{warningbox}
\textbf{Finding}: $\sim$80\% of the F1 loss across \emph{all} items
was caused by a single bug in the evaluation script, not in the
extraction pipeline!
\end{warningbox}

The \texttt{strip\_html()} function in \texttt{evaluate.py} was
\textbf{not decoding HTML entities} before comparing text.

\begin{analogybox}
Imagine two people writing the word ``caf\'e''---one writes it normally,
the other writes \texttt{caf\&eacute;} with an accent stored as a
special code. They mean the same thing, but a computer comparing
them letter-by-letter would say they're different!
\end{analogybox}

\subsection{The Entity Encoding Problem}

HTML represents special characters two ways:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Unicode character}: the byte \texttt{\textbackslash xa0}
        (what ground truth stored)
  \item \textbf{HTML entity}: the text \texttt{\&\#160;} (what
        predictions contained)
\end{itemize}

Both encode the \emph{same} non-breaking space. But
\texttt{strip\_html()} stripped tags without converting entities.
So ``\texttt{\&}'', ``\texttt{\#}'', ``\texttt{1}'', ``\texttt{6}'',
``\texttt{0}'', ``\texttt{;}'' were counted as six characters instead
of one space---inflating prediction length and creating phantom
mismatches.


% ===============================================================
\section{Fix 1: HTML Entity Decoding}\label{sec:fix1}
% ===============================================================

\subsection{What We Changed}

A single line was added to \texttt{evaluate.py}:

\begin{lstlisting}[language=Python,caption={The one-line fix in \texttt{strip\_html()}.}]
def strip_html(html: str) -> str:
    text = re.sub(r'<[^>]+>', ' ', html)
    text = html_module.unescape(text)  # <-- NEW LINE
    text = re.sub(r'\s+', ' ',
                  text.replace('\u00a0', ' ')).strip()
    return text
\end{lstlisting}

The call to \texttt{html.unescape()} converts all HTML entities to
their actual Unicode characters:
\texttt{\&\#160;} $\to$ non-breaking space,
\texttt{\&amp;} $\to$ \texttt{\&},
\texttt{\&lt;} $\to$ \texttt{<}, etc.

\subsection{Why This Is Not Bias}

This corrects the \emph{measurement}, not the predictions. The metric
should measure content overlap; HTML entity encoding is a serialization
detail (like \texttt{42} vs.\ \texttt{0x2A}).

\section{Fix 2: Item 16 Pass-Through}\label{sec:fix2}
% ===============================================================

\subsection{The Problem}

Item~16 (``Form 10-K Summary'') is optional. Most companies write
``None'' or ``Not applicable.'' The old code used a complex function
\texttt{\_truncate\_placeholder\_item16()} that tried to detect
placeholders and truncate the HTML.

This was problematic: ground truth stores the \emph{full} HTML slice
anchor-to-anchor, including page breaks, numbers, and trailing
whitespace. Our truncation cut this material, causing mismatches.

\subsection{Ground Truth Patterns for Item 16}

\begin{itemize}
  \item $\sim$20 files: GT has \textbf{empty string}
  \item $\sim$65 files: GT has \textbf{short HTML} (``None'' plus footers)
  \item $\sim$21 files: GT has \textbf{long content} to end of document
\end{itemize}

The truncation function was wrong for all three groups---sometimes
removing too much, sometimes not enough.

\subsection{The Solution}

Replace complex truncation with simple pass-through:

\begin{lstlisting}[language=Python,caption={Item 16: before and after.}]
# Before: complex truncation
result[key] = _truncate_placeholder_item16(html_slice)

# After: uniform treatment
result[key] = html_slice
\end{lstlisting}

\begin{analogybox}
It's like cutting chapters out of a book: for Chapter~16 you tried
to guess how much to include based on whether it was ``important.''
The fix: just cut from Chapter~16's heading to Chapter~17's heading,
exactly like every other chapter!
\end{analogybox}

\subsection{Why This Is Not Bias}

This change \textbf{removes} a special-case heuristic. Item~16 is
now treated identically to items 1--15. Less special-casing = less
bias.


% ===============================================================
\section{Bias Analysis}\label{sec:bias}
% ===============================================================

\begin{analogybox}
In school, there's a difference between studying hard and finding
the answer key. We need to make sure our improvements are like
studying hard (understanding the problem better) and not like
peeking at the answers.
\end{analogybox}

Bias would mean unfairly improving scores without genuinely improving
extraction quality---e.g., hardcoding specific file answers, tuning
to match ground truth, or inflating the evaluator.

\begin{table}[htbp]
\centering
\caption{Bias checklist for both changes.}\label{tab:bias}
\begin{tabu}{Xcc}
\toprule
\textbf{Question} & \textbf{Fix 1} & \textbf{Fix 2} \\
\midrule
Changes predictions? & No & Slightly \\
Favors specific files? & No & No \\
Is it principled? & Yes & Yes \\
Generalizes to new data? & Yes & Yes \\
Adds special-case logic? & No & No (removes it) \\
\midrule
\textbf{Introduces bias?} & \textbf{No} & \textbf{No} \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabu}
\end{table}

\begin{resultbox}
\textbf{Verdict}: No bias was introduced. Fix~1 corrects a measurement
bug. Fix~2 removes an overfitted heuristic. Both are uniform across
all files and would generalize to new data.
\end{resultbox}

\section{Remaining Challenges}\label{sec:remaining}
% ===============================================================

\subsection{Item 16: Still the Weakest (86.4\%)}

Ground truth is inconsistent for item16---sometimes empty, sometimes
short HTML, sometimes the full remainder of the filing. No single
heuristic works for all cases.

\subsection{Outlier Files (F1 $<$ 70\%)}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{0000063908} (McDonald's): Extractor misses 5 section
        boundaries; content from items 3, 5, 6, 7, 9b gets absorbed.
  \item \textbf{0001193125-20-048303}: GT merges sections into 7
        blobs; our 21-item prediction is actually \emph{more correct}.
  \item \textbf{0000721371}: Missing boundaries for items 6, 7, 7a;
        anomalous 15.7M-character signatures entry in GT.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Signatures (92.4\%)}

Our pipeline outputs empty for all signatures (correct for 97/105
files). The 8 files with massive GT entries (12M--80M chars) include
the entire rest of the filing---unmatchable without a fundamentally
different approach.

\subsection{Ground Truth Quality}

Some GT entries contain annotation errors: item1b containing item1a
content, item4 spanning hundreds of financial pages, inconsistent
placeholder treatment. These set a ceiling on achievable F1.

\section{Lessons Learned}\label{sec:lessons}
% ===============================================================

\begin{description}
  \item[Measure twice, cut once.]
    The biggest improvement came from fixing the \emph{measurement},
    not the extraction. Before optimizing your model, verify your
    metrics are correct. A broken ruler sends you down the wrong path.

  \item[Simple is often better.]
    The item16 truncation heuristic was complex and fragile. Replacing
    it with uniform pass-through improved scores. When in doubt,
    remove special cases rather than adding more.

  \item[Analyze before optimizing.]
    Parallel analysis agents revealed that most items shared the same
    root cause. Without deep analysis, we might have written complex
    per-item fixes instead of one entity-decoding fix.

  \item[Ground truth is not gospel.]
    Several ``failures'' were cases where our extraction was more
    correct than the GT. Understanding GT quality sets realistic
    expectations and prevents overfitting to annotation errors.

  \item[Beware of bias.]
    Every change was evaluated: Does it apply uniformly? Is it
    principled? Would it generalize? Both changes passed all tests.
\end{description}
